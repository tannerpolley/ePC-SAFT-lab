\begin{abstract}
Lithium is a strategic element for energy storage. Extraction from surface water is a promising method for the purification of lithium. Synthetic model brines containing lithium and sodium ions were prepared for extraction experiments. A hydrophobic deep eutectic solvent (HDES) was synthesized using tetrabutylammonium chloride (TBAC) as the hydrogen bond acceptor (HBA) and decanoic acid (DA) as the hydrogen bond donor (HBD). Trioctylphosphine oxide (TOPO) was chosen as the coextractant. Fourier transform infrared (FT-IR) and ${ }^{1} \mathrm{H}$ NMR analyses confirmed the physical interactions between the HBD and HBA. Response surface methodology was used to optimize the operational parameters for lithium extraction percentage and selectivity. The organic phase containing TBAC(1)/DA(2) and $10 \mathrm{wt} \%$ of TOPO at $T= 23.0^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ and $\mathrm{pH}=10.4$ demonstrated the highest extraction performance. \section*{- INTRODUCTION}

Energy is a vital requirement for humans. In recent decades, fossil fuels have become a predominant source of energy. Today, with the progress of industrial demand, as well as the increasing global population, reliance on fossil fuels faces significant challenges due to the shortage of natural resources and environmental pollution resulting from their extraction and combustion. ${ }^{1}$ One solution to this issue is the utilization of alternative energy sources, such as nuclear energy or sustainable and renewable energy. Effective use of renewable energy requires storage under low-demand conditions for utilization when demand is higher. ${ }^{2}$

Lithium is one of the most vital elements for energy storage. The form of lithium batteries facilitates usage across various conditions and devices. ${ }^{3}$ Lithium's other significant applications in nuclear industries, ${ }^{4}$ ceramics industries, ${ }^{5}$ solid-state electrolytes, ${ }^{6}$ industrial lubricants, ${ }^{7}$ and electric vehicles, ${ }^{8}$ highlight the importance of securing and deploying it as a strategic element.

The major sources of lithium are ores and surface waters such as salt lake brine and seawater (because of the high solubility of lithium salts in water). Seawater and salt lake brine have low lithium concentrations. However, due to their enormous abundance, ${ }^{9}$ they are a tremendous source of lithium if properly separated.

There are several methods for separating lithium from water such as precipitation, ${ }^{10}$ ion exchange, ${ }^{11}$ membrane separation, ${ }^{12}$ chromatography, ${ }^{13}$ adsorption, ${ }^{14}$ and liquid-liquid extraction. ${ }^{15}$ Among these methods, liquid-liquid extraction from brines, which is usually performed under ambient conditions, is much simpler and is the most accepted method because of its low energy consumption. The main challenge in liquid-liquid extraction is to find a proper solvent. ${ }^{16}$

A variety of organic solvents have been developed for lithium extraction such as $\beta$-diketones, ${ }^{17}$ crown ethers, ${ }^{18}$ tributyl phosphate, and trioctylphosphine oxide solution in organic solvents like kerosene or toluene. ${ }^{19}$ These organic solvents showed good separation efficiencies in some cases like TBP-kerosene ${ }^{20}$ and benzoyltrifluoroacetone (HBTA)-toluene. ${ }^{19}$ Organic solvents have a good separation ability for lithium. Ionic liquids (IL) are alternatives to common organic solvents. The low volatility of ILs is advantageous for extraction systems because it reduces environmental pollution and solvent loss at relatively high temperatures. However, challenges in the synthesis process lead to high costs. On the other hand, their high viscosity limits their application in separation processes. ${ }^{22}$ Additionally, ion exchange between the cation of IL and lithium makes the regeneration of solvents difficult. ${ }^{23}$

Deep eutectic solvents (DESs), as a new generation of solvents, are mainly composed of a hydrogen bond donor (HBD), like urea or alcohols, and a hydrogen bond acceptor (HBA), like quaternary ammonium or phosphonium salts. ${ }^{24}$ Due to the high variety of materials that can be used to produce DESs, these solvents can be considered as tunable agents and can be used for many different purposes. DESs have the benefits of ILs such as low vapor pressure and nonflammability. Other advantages over ILs are biodegradability, lower viscosity, and lower cost. ${ }^{25}$ These advantages make them more practical in many fields of science and engineering, including desulfurization, ${ }^{26}$ electrodeposition, ${ }^{27}$ reaction media, ${ }^{28}$ gas absorption, ${ }^{29-31}$ and extraction. The reason for this is the solubility of target compounds like drugs, ${ }^{32}$ aromatics, ${ }^{33}$ and metals from various sources in DESs. ${ }^{34}$ In metal extraction, we emphasize the extraction of lithium from seawater and brine using hydrophobic deep eutectic solvents (HDES), which is the main goal of this research. Since DES, as a solvent, is in contact with water, the importance of hydrophobicity is very remarkable. Higher hydrophobicity results in easier separation of the phases and a reduction in the loss of the solvent. The extraction of lithium using HDESs has been investigated by researchers in recent years. $\beta$-Diketones were mainly used as HBD for the synthesis of HDES with trioctylphosphine oxide (TOPO) and tributyl phosphate (TBP) as HBAs. ${ }^{35}$ Table 1 represents a summary of published research investigating the lithium extraction performance of HDESs.

Considering the relatively high cost of some materials, especially HBTA, finding a cost-effective DES for lithium extraction is necessary. Carboxylic acids with long hydrocarbon chains can be good HBDs due to their hydrophobicity and the potential of hydrogen in the COOH group to perform an ion exchange reaction with lithium ions. ${ }^{36}$ Quaternary ammonium salts can selectively interact with lithium ions due to their ability to form strong coordination complexes. The positive
charge on the ammonium ion enhances its affinity toward negatively charged or polar species, which can help in selectively extracting lithium over other cations like sodium or potassium. ${ }^{9}$ The use of these salts can enhance the solubility of lithium salts in nonaqueous solvents, making it easier to extract lithium from solutions. Quaternary ammonium salts often participate in forming micelles or complexes, which can stabilize lithium ions in solution, improving their availability for extraction. ${ }^{39}$ In the context of DES, quaternary ammonium salts serve as HBAs. When combined with suitable HBDs, they can create a solvent system that is highly efficient for lithium extraction. DES can exhibit tunable properties based on the combination of HBA and HBD, allowing for customization according to specific extraction needs. ${ }^{36}$ The application of quaternary ammonium salts for lithium extraction can contribute to more sustainable practices. They can sometimes reduce the need for harsh solvents or reactants, aligning with the growing emphasis on environmentally friendly extraction methods. ${ }^{36}$ For exploring the effects of operational parameters on lithium extraction, one factor at a time method has been applied by researchers. ${ }^{19,21,35-38}$ This method is not a comprehensive approach because it cannot predict the reciprocal effects of parameters simultaneously.

This study focused on developing a cost-effective and biodegradable HDES. For this purpose, decanoic acid (DA), due to its long hydrocarbon chain, and tetrabutylammonium chloride (TBAC) were used as the HBD and HBA, respectively. The produced HDES was then used as the primary solvent for the extraction of lithium from the synthetic model brine. Zubeir et al. ${ }^{40}$ synthesized the same DES and used it in carbon dioxide capture as a green and biodegradable solvent. van Osch et al. ${ }^{41}$ investigated the physical properties of TBAC(1)/DA(2) DES, including its melting point ( -11.95 ${ }^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ ) and hydrophobicity ( 69380 ppm water in the organic phase after mixing with water). In this study, the main reasons for using DA/TBAC DES for lithium extraction are its hydrophobicity, lower cost, and biodegradability compared to other DESs reported in the literature. In order to increase the extraction efficiency, trioctylphosphine oxide, tributyl phosphate, and bis(2-ethylhexyl)phosphate were examined as coextractants in relatively lower amounts than the DES. For investigating the selectivity of the HDES for lithium extraction, a synthetic model brine containing lithium and sodium (as competing ions) ions was prepared. This was due to the abundance of sodium ions in the surface water and alkaline brines. To study the effect of operational parameters on lithium extraction and selectivity, response surface method-

Phase Equilibrium Experiments. To determine the separation of lithium under equilibrium conditions, extraction experiments were conducted in a two-shell self-made flask (the same as mentioned in the DES synthesis section and shown in Figure 1) connected to a water circulator and a condenser. A suitable amount of the synthesized DES with an equal mass of aqueous LiCl solution was mixed at a specific temperature for 2 h to guarantee thermodynamic equilibrium. The aqueous solution was prepared with a lithium concentration of 1500 ppmw in deionized water. Ammonia solution was used to regulate the pH of the solution to 9.0 using a pH meter (Istek PH240L).

The mixture was then centrifuged (UNIVERSAL) for 5 min at a speed of 3000 rpm to separate the DES phase from the aqueous phase. Then, the aqueous phase was sampled using a needled syringe, weighed, and analyzed using ICP-OES (Varian, 730-ES) to determine the concentration of lithium. The concentration of lithium in the DES phase was calculated using mass balance. The extraction efficiency percentage ( $E \%$ ) was then calculated using eq 1 .

Since lithium is not always the only cation in aqueous solutions, it is necessary to investigate the equilibrium with a competing cation, such as sodium or potassium, to evaluate the solvent's selectivity. In this work, sodium was considered as the competing impurity, and the selectivity of the solvent was calculated using eq 2.

$$
\begin{align*}
E_{i}(\%) & =\frac{M_{i_{\text {ini }}}-M_{i_{\mathrm{f}}}}{M_{i_{\text {ini }}}} \times 100 \\
& =\frac{C_{i_{\text {ini }}} \times V_{\text {ini }}-C_{i_{\mathrm{f}}} \times V_{\mathrm{f}}}{C_{i_{\text {ini }}} \times V_{\text {ini }}} \times 100 \tag{1}
\end{align*}
$$


In eq $1, M_{i}$ is the mass of the lithium or sodium, $C_{i}$ is the concentration of the metals (g/lit), and $V$ (lit) is the volume of the aqueous phase. Subscripts ini and f represent the initial and final states of the system, respectively.

$$
\begin{equation*}
\text { selectivity }=\frac{E_{\mathrm{Li}}}{E_{\mathrm{Na}}} \tag{2}
\end{equation*}
$$


Regeneration of the Organic Phase. One of the important features of any solvent in the extraction process is its reusability. Since increasing the pH results in more lithium extraction, the regeneration of the organic phase and stripping of the lithium were carried out using a strong acid solution. After extraction, the DES phase was first separated and then mixed vigorously with an equal mass of a3 molar HCl solution for 1 h to guarantee thermodynamic equilibrium. Then, the mixture was centrifuged to separate the regenerated organic phase from the lithium-rich aqueous phase. The regenerated organic phase was then mixed with fresh aqueous feed. This procedure was repeated 5 times and the reusability of the organic solvent was explored.

Design of Experiments. Design of experiments (DOE) was used to explore the importance of independent parameters on lithium extraction and selectivity. As response variables and to find the optimized values of independent parameters, four parameters, including temperature, pH , coextractant concentration, and the sodium-to-lithium mass ratio were investigated using response surface methodology (RSM). This method is widely used for DOE applied in basic science and engineering, including chemistry, ${ }^{45}$ chemical engineering, ${ }^{46}$ and material science. ${ }^{47}$

Defining a reasonable range for all parameters is necessary to apply RSM. Since liquid-phase extraction systems usually favor low temperatures, a near-ambient temperature range of 20.0 to $40.0^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ was selected. The pH of the aqueous phase is an important parameter that has a major impact on lithium separation. Since DESs have carboxylic functional groups, a higher pH is favorable for lithium extraction. Therefore, the pH of the aqueous phase ranged from 9.0 to 11.0 in the DOE. The main reason for selecting pH 11.0 as the maximum value was the appearance of a third liquid phase and emulsification. A stable emulsion appeared in the systems when the pH of the aqueous phase was higher than 11.0 .

Extraction Mechanism. To determine the mechanism of lithium extraction, the FT-IR spectra of the organic phase were obtained before and after the extraction process. The results are depicted in Figure 11.

The main peaks are identified by dashed lines, and the shifts are indicated in magenta. The peak at $1730 \mathrm{~cm}^{-1}$ corresponds to the $\mathrm{C}=\mathrm{O}$ bond in decanoic acid, which is red-shifted to $1672 \mathrm{~cm}^{-1}$ in the lithium-loaded organic phase. This shift suggests the successful transfer of lithium into the organic phase. ${ }^{36}$ The peak associated with the $\mathrm{P}=\mathrm{O}$ group in trioctylphosphine oxide (TOPO) is observed at $1149 \mathrm{~cm}^{-1}$; however, due to coordination with lithium, this peak is shifted to $1110 \mathrm{~cm}^{-1} .^{19}$ The extraction of lithium via deep eutectic solvents (DES) has contributed to the weakening of the hydrogen bonds between the DES components. This weakening is reflected in the redshift of the $\mathrm{C}=\mathrm{O}$ group, as well as a shift in the $\mathrm{C}-\mathrm{O}-\mathrm{H}$ group from 1470 to $1454 \mathrm{~cm}^{-1}$, indicating an exchange of lithium ions with $\mathrm{H}^{+}$from decanoic
acid. As TOPO participated in the extraction, the frequency of the $\mathrm{Li}-\mathrm{O}$ bond increased, resulting in a shorter bond length. This indicates a stronger combination of the lithium atom and oxygen atom. The ion exchange reaction is as follows

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \text { TBAC } \cdots \mathrm{R}-\mathrm{COOH}_{(\text {org })}+\mathrm{Li}_{(\mathrm{aq})}^{+} \\
& \xrightarrow{\mathrm{OH}^{-}} \text {TBAC } \cdots \mathrm{R}-\mathrm{COOLi}_{(\mathrm{org})}+\mathrm{H}_{(\mathrm{aq})}^{+}
\end{aligned}
$$


The exchange of lithium ions with protons ( $\mathrm{H}^{+}$) from decanoic acid is critical for the extraction mechanism and indicates a competitive environment in which lithium aims to displace weaker $\mathrm{H}^{+}$bonds, thus enriching the organic phase with lithium. TOPO acts as a bidentate ligand that coordinates with lithium ions through its oxygen atoms. This coordination enhances the stability of the lithium-TOPO complex. ${ }^{39,44}$ The increased frequency of the $\mathrm{Li}-\mathrm{O}$ bond suggests stronger interactions, which may arise from the effective orbital overlap between the lithium's s-orbitals and the lone pairs on the oxygen in the TOPO molecule. TOPO coordinates with lithium, and the resulting $\mathrm{Li}-\mathrm{O}$ bond undergoes a redshift in the vibrational frequency and a reduction in the bond length. This phenomenon indicates that the electronegativity differences and steric effects inherent in the TOPO structure led to a more covalent character of the bond, enhancing the stability of the complex formed during the extraction process. ${ }^{39,44}$

Hanada et al. ${ }^{19}$ obtained an extraction efficiency of less than $2 \%$ for pure TOPO in toluene. In our work, the extraction efficiency of the DES alone was $15 \%$. The extraction efficiency of the mixture containing $90 \mathrm{wt} \% \mathrm{DES}+10 \mathrm{wt} \% \mathrm{TOPO}$ was $48 \%$. A comparison of these observations confirms that TOPO has better performance when used with DES instead of toluene as a solvent. Since toluene has almost no tendency to extract lithium, the extraction efficiency of TOPO in these solvents is very low. However, when DES is used as a solvent, the system exhibits a synergistic effect between DES and TOPO for lithium extraction.

TOPO coordinates with lithium, and the resulting $\mathrm{Li}-\mathrm{O}$ bond undergoes a red shift in the vibrational frequency and a reduction in the bond length. This phenomenon indicates that the electronegativity differences and steric effects inherent in the TOPO structure led to a more covalent character of the bond, enhancing the stability of the complex formed during the extraction process. ${ }^{19,35,39,44,51}$

The molecular mechanism involved in lithium extraction with TOPO involves a complex interplay of coordination chemistry, hydrogen bonding dynamics, solvent effects, and thermodynamic principles. Each of these elements contributes to the overall effectiveness of lithium extraction from aqueous sources, ultimately enhancing our understanding of how modifying solvent systems and ligands can optimize the extraction processes. ${ }^{39,51-54}$

A comparison of the extraction efficiency of lithium against other cations (like $\mathrm{Na}, \mathrm{Ca}, \mathrm{K}$, and Mg ) can reveal that the TOPO's affinity for lithium differs from that of other ions. The molecular size, charge density, and hydration energies of these ions can inform why lithium might preferentially coordinate with TOPO over the others, leading to higher selectivity. ${ }^{44,51-54}$

The extraction process appears to weaken the hydrogen bonds between the DES components. This weakening affects the overall solubility and dynamics of the ion interactions within the solution. The shift in the $\mathrm{C}=\mathrm{O}$ and $\mathrm{C}-\mathrm{O}-\mathrm{H}$
stretching frequencies (in Figure 11) points to changes in hydrogen bonding that facilitate the dissociation of $\mathrm{H}^{+}$ions in favor of lithium. ${ }^{51-54}$

Lithium Extraction with Multiple Competing Cations. Natural brines containing lithium have more cations in addition to sodium and lithium. Bazamad et al. ${ }^{42}$ investigated the geochemical behavior and exploration potential of lithium in brines. They conducted a case study of a Bam salt plug in the Zagros Zone in southern Iran. Based on their research, the composition of brine in one region is presented in Table 8. We prepared a synthetic brine containing cations with high concentrations, as in the real brine ( $\mathrm{Li}, \mathrm{Na}, \mathrm{K}, \mathrm{Mg}$, and Ca ), to investigate the performance of our HDES. The extraction experiment was conducted under the obtained optimal conditions, and the extraction efficiencies for all cations were calculated using eq 1. The results are presented in Table 8.

The extraction efficiency for lithium was $51.63 \%$ under the optimal operating conditions, while those for $\mathrm{Na}, \mathrm{K}, \mathrm{Mg}$, and Ca were 9.97, 3.11, 4.38, and $2.29 \%$, respectively. Based on
these results, it can be confirmed that $90 \mathrm{wt} \% \mathrm{DES}+10 \mathrm{wt} \%$ TOPO can be used to recover lithium from natural alkaline brines, including other competing cations. By performing extraction in multiple stages, much higher extraction efficiencies can be achieved.

The main reasons for the higher selectivity of lithium over other cations are the cation charge density, size, binding affinity differences, ion interactions, and solvation effects. These concepts are discussed below.

Charge Density. Lithium ( $\mathrm{Li}^{+}$), being a smaller and more highly charged ion $(+1)$, has a higher charge density compared to sodium ( $\mathrm{Na}^{+}$), calcium ( $\mathrm{Ca}^{2+}$ ), potassium ( $\mathrm{K}^{+}$), and magnesium $\left(\mathrm{Mg}^{2+}\right)$. This affects its interaction with ligands like TOPO, making it more favorable for coordination due to stronger electrostatic attraction. ${ }^{39,55}$

Ionic Radius Consideration. The ionic radius of lithium (approximately $0.76 \AA$ ) is smaller than that of sodium ( $1.02 \AA$ ) and similar to that of magnesium ( $0.72 \AA$ ). The ability of TOPO to selectively coordinate $\mathrm{Li}^{+}$over these larger ions can also be influenced by size-exclusion effects and steric hindrance. ${ }^{56,57}$

Coordination with TOPO. The binding affinity of $\mathrm{Li}^{+}$to phosphine oxides like TOPO is typically stronger than that of larger cations due to its optimal size and charge. The $\mathrm{P}=\mathrm{O}$ groups in TOPO can form a stable coordination complex with $\mathrm{Li}^{+}$, resulting in characteristic shifts in vibrational frequencies, as discussed earlier. ${ }^{39,44}$

Competitive Binding. $\mathrm{Ca}^{2+}, \mathrm{K}^{+}$, and $\mathrm{Mg}^{2+}$ may compete with $\mathrm{Li}^{+}$for the same coordination sites. The higher charges of $\mathrm{Ca}^{2+}$ and $\mathrm{Mg}^{2+}$ can lead to stronger ionic interactions; however, their larger size may hinder effective coordination with TOPO compared to $\mathrm{Li}^{+}$. ${ }^{39,44,55}$

Ion Pairing. Divalent cations such as $\mathrm{Ca}^{2+}$ and $\mathrm{Mg}^{2+}$ may form stronger ion pairs with solvent molecules, reducing their availability for coordination with organic ligands. This can affect the extraction dynamics, as these cations can create solvation shells that prevent lithium from effectively interacting with TOPO. ${ }^{39}$

Hydration Energy. The hydration enthalpy also plays a crucial role in selective extraction. $\mathrm{Li}^{+}$has higher hydration energy than $\mathrm{Na}^{+}, \mathrm{K}^{+}$, and $\mathrm{Ca}^{2+}$. This implies that $\mathrm{Li}^{+}$may require less energy to be released from its hydrated form, promoting its extraction into the organic phase. ${ }^{44,55,58}$

The HDES was stable in extracting lithium from water with negligible loss of solvent due to its high hydrophobicity. The extraction mechanism was determined by using the FT-IR spectra. The ion exchange reaction between lithium in the aqueous phase and hydrogen in the organic phase, as well as the complexation of lithium with TOPO, were dominant. Finally, the performance of the HDES was examined for lithium extraction from a synthetic brine similar to the natural brines of southern Iran (containing lithium, sodium, potassium, magnesium, and calcium). The extraction efficiency for lithium reached $51.36 \%$, which was much higher than that of the other ions.

Based on the results of this research, we conclude that TBAC(1)/DA(2) with $10.0 \mathrm{wt} \% \mathrm{TOPO}$ as a coextractant can be used as a cost-effective and biodegradable solvent for selective lithium extraction from alkaline brines.
